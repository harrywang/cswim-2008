Business intelligence (BI) refers to the application of data mining and other intelligent computing techniques in supporting business decision making. As BI has been attracting active, fascinating research in recent years, some of the outcomes are starting to find successful applications in practice. At the same time, emerging problems arising along with new business phenomena, such as electronic commerce and mobile commerce, are generating new demands and opportunities for research. To investigate practical challenges and identify future research directions, this discussion panel will present the state of the art of the BI area and the gaps between research and practice. The objective of the panel is to foster communication between scholars in China and those overseas, as well as between academic researchers and practicing professionals. The particular topics discussed in the panel include the following.

  • Business Intelligence in the Cyber World
    • The application of business intelligence in the Internet-based cyber world is characterized as being distributed, real-time, concurrent, and dynamic. Current viable topics in this cutting-edge area include online target marketing, anti-money-laundering, virtual market, service computing, and anti-Internet-fraud. Accurate, real-time decisions based on vast amount of data on the Internet are imperative for any e-commerce company to survive and succeed in this ˇ°New Marketˇ±. Business Intelligence enables companies to access vast supplies of data and turn them into information that is transformed into real knowledge for decisions and actions. There are new research challenges from the cyber world to business intelligence, such as concurrent data mining, active learning, and distributed temporal data analysis. These research problems stimulate the revolutionary changes in the latest information technology including hardware, software, and the ideologies.

  • Cost-sensitive Learning
    • In real-world predictive data mining (classification or regression) problems, different types of prediction errors often incur asymmetric costs. Classifying a problematic bank as a healthy one, thus allowing the problems go unnoticed, has much more serious consequences than doing the reverse. Under-forecasting the loan loss amount of a bank, thus leading the bank to have insufficient reserves, is much more costly than over-forecasting the same amount. Such practical problems demand cost-sensitive learning methods that attempt to minimize the overall misprediction cost rather than simple performance measures like error rate and squared error. What approaches can be taken to make basic learning methods cost-sensitive? What implications do these approaches have on different types of learning methods, such as linear and logistic regression, classification and regression trees, and neural networks? In practical situations where the costs cannot be precisely pinned down, how should tools be designed to help decision makers make tradeoffs on different prediction errors?

  • Fusion of Human Knowledge and Machine Learning
    • Machine learning techniques have been applied to solve predictive data mining problems for a variety of applications. In many application domains, there also exists human expert knowledge that could have a bearing on the effectiveness of prediction models. Bank loan officers have expertise in assessing the credit of a loan applicant. Banking regulators use systems, such as the CAMEL (Capital adequacy, Asset quality, Management quality, Earnings ability, and Liquidity position) framework, in evaluating the soundness of banks. When both historical data and domain knowledge exist, how can they be combined to build more effective decision models? What forms of domain knowledge are available in practical applications? How do these forms of knowledge impact different types of learning techniques.
